Why post-quantum?

Quantum computers capable of breaking elliptic curve cryptography are no longer a theoretical concern. Google's Willow chip crossed the quantum error correction threshold in 2024. NIST finalized post-quantum cryptography standards the same year. The NSA requires all national security systems to migrate to post-quantum algorithms by 2035.

Every deployed privacy protocol—Zcash, Tornado Cash, Aztec, Penumbra—relies on elliptic curves for note encryption, key agreement, or signatures. When those curves break, the transaction graphs will be fully exposed retroactively.

Why now?

Adversaries are already collecting encrypted blockchain data for future decryption. The NSA, CISA, and FBI have jointly warned about this "harvest now, decrypt later" strategy.

For financial privacy, the shelf life of transaction data is effectively infinite. Shielded notes on systems using elliptic-curve Diffie-Hellman—like Zcash Orchard's Pallas-curve DH for note encryption—will have their encrypted memos and note values exposed once a quantum computer solves the discrete log. Address reuse compounds the damage: shared diversified keys let an attacker correlate transactions and partially reconstruct the payment graph.

The time to deploy post-quantum privacy is before the data is harvested, not after the quantum computer arrives.

No elliptic curves.
Anywhere.

TzEL doesn't patch curves with a post-quantum layer—it eliminates them entirely. BLAKE2s for hashing, ML-KEM-768 for encryption, hash-based one-time signatures for spend authorization, and STARKs for proofs.

Fund safety relies on hash collision resistance and STARK soundness. Note confidentiality relies on ML-KEM (NIST FIPS 203) and authenticated encryption. No component depends on the hardness of discrete log or factoring.

commitment  = BLAKE2s(d, v, rcm, tag)
nullifier   = BLAKE2s(nk, cm, pos)
memo       = ML-KEM-768 + ChaCha20
spend auth = hash-based OTS verified in-STARK
proof      = recursive STARK (Cairo + Stwo)

Sign on your hardware wallet.
Prove anywhere.

TzEL separates signing from proving. Even the most lightweight device—a hardware wallet, a phone, a smart card—can authorize a transaction by producing a single hash-based signature. The heavy proof generation is outsourced to any untrusted machine.

The signature is verified inside the STARK circuit. A malicious prover cannot redirect funds—the signature binds to the exact outputs. The prover never sees your signing key, and the chain never sees auth leaves, public keys, or spend signatures.

Run the prover on your own machine, or delegate to a third-party service running a TEE. In the worst case—a TEE breach—the attacker learns which transaction you made, but still cannot steal funds or forge spends. Proving is a pure computation with no secret key access.

hardware wallet:
  sign(sighash) → wots_signature

untrusted prover:
  witness + signature → STARK proof

on-chain verifier sees:
  proof, nullifiers, commitments
  no keys, no signatures, no auth leaves

Your transactions.
Your choice who sees them.

Private notes and memos are encrypted on-chain by default. The chain still reveals transaction shape and timing, but not note plaintexts, auth paths, or spend signatures.

TzEL gives you fine-grained viewing keys you can share selectively. Give an auditor read access to a single address. Let a portfolio tracker see balances without spending power. Disclose exactly what you choose, to exactly who you choose, and nothing more.

viewing key:   read balances + memos
spending key:  full control

you choose who gets what
